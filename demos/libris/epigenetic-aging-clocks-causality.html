Do DNA-methylation aging clocks measure causal aging, or just correlate with it?

Summary

DNA-methylation (DNAm) aging clocks are among the most reproducible biomarkers of aging, but the causal interpretation remains much weaker than the predictive one. The strongest conclusion is that Horvath, PhenoAge, GrimAge, and DunedinPACE measure aging-related state and risk, not a single proven causal aging process. They are useful because they compress many age-, exposure-, tissue-, cell-composition-, disease-, and mortality-related signals into a number. That number can predict chronological age, mortality, and functional decline. But prediction is not causation.

The evidence that clock changes are drivers of aging is currently limited and mixed. Interventions can move some clock readouts, especially pace-of-aging measures such as DunedinPACE, but most interventions are broad and do not show that the methylation changes used by the clocks are themselves sufficient or necessary causes of aging phenotypes. Recent work attempting to enrich clocks for causality argues that standard epigenetic clocks may mix damaging changes with adaptive or compensatory changes, which directly undermines the simple view that all clock acceleration is harmful causal aging.

Key findings

1. First-generation clocks establish biomarker validity, not causality

The Horvath multi-tissue clock was trained to predict chronological age from methylation at 353 CpGs across tissues. Its significance is that methylation contains a highly reproducible age signal. However, a model trained on chronological age does not by itself identify the causes of aging; it may learn downstream consequences, tissue composition shifts, developmental residue, mitotic history, or correlated maintenance processes.

Established: Horvath-style DNAm age is a robust age-correlated biomarker. Not established: the CpGs in the model are causal drivers of organismal aging.

2. Second-generation clocks deliberately encode morbidity and mortality risk

PhenoAge and GrimAge are often more predictive of disease and death than chronological-age clocks, but that partly reflects how they were built. DNAm PhenoAge estimates a clinical-biomarker/mortality-derived phenotypic age. GrimAge uses DNAm surrogates for plasma proteins and smoking pack-years to predict time-to-death and age-related outcomes. These clocks therefore measure mortality-relevant biology more directly than Horvath, but they are not “pure” measures of an intrinsic aging program.

Established: PhenoAge and GrimAge have stronger healthspan/mortality associations than many first-generation clocks. Interpretation: this supports clinical predictive value, not direct proof that clock CpGs drive aging.

3. DunedinPACE is closer to a pace-of-aging measure, but still a proxy

DunedinPACE was trained from longitudinal within-person change in multi-system physiology in the Dunedin cohort. This is conceptually stronger than fitting chronological age, because the target is observed physiological decline. Still, the DNAm signature is a proxy trained to estimate a phenotype. It may reflect immune status, inflammation, exposure history, and other correlates of decline rather than causal methylation lesions.

Established: DunedinPACE is designed to track the pace of physiological aging better than age-prediction clocks. Not established: changing DunedinPACE CpGs directly changes the pace of biological aging.

4. Mortality prediction is real, but it is predictive validity

Large cohort/meta-analytic evidence shows DNAm age acceleration predicts all-cause mortality beyond chronological age. This is one of the strongest results in the field. But mortality prediction can arise from many non-causal routes: clocks may mark disease burden, smoking, inflammation, cell-type composition, socioeconomic exposure, or frailty.

Established: DNAm age acceleration contains mortality-risk information. Causal inference status: observational prediction alone cannot distinguish driver from passenger.

5. Interventional evidence exists but is still limited

The best human interventional evidence is not that clocks cause aging, but that clock readouts are responsive to interventions.

  • TRIIM: A small open-label thymus-regeneration intervention reported reversal of epigenetic age and immunosenescent trends. Because it was small, uncontrolled, and multi-component, it is weak causal evidence.
  • CALERIE: A randomized caloric-restriction trial found effects on DNAm biological-aging measures, especially DunedinPACE. This is stronger evidence that some clock measures respond to an intervention plausibly affecting aging biology. However, it still does not show that methylation-clock CpGs are mediators rather than readouts.
  • Exercise / partial reprogramming / animal studies: These show that epigenetic age can shift with broad interventions. They are mechanistically suggestive but usually alter many cellular programs simultaneously.

Established: some interventions can move clock readouts. Speculative: clock movement mediates the health benefits of those interventions.

6. Methodological critiques are serious

Several critiques weaken a simple causal interpretation:

  1. Training target problem: clocks trained on chronological age, mortality, or clinical biomarkers inherit the assumptions and biases of those targets.
  2. Confounding and reverse causation: environmental, social, disease, inflammatory, and behavioral factors associate with clock acceleration, but most evidence is observational.
  3. Cell-composition effects: blood-based clocks may partly reflect immune-cell proportions and immune aging.
  4. Tissue specificity: a clock trained in one tissue may not reflect causal aging biology in another.
  5. Heterogeneous CpGs: clock CpGs may include upstream drivers, downstream damage markers, adaptive responses, and neutral passengers.
  6. Outcome circularity: GrimAge and PhenoAge predict mortality partly because mortality-related information was built into them.
  7. Lack of sufficiency tests: few studies directly edit clock CpGs and test whether aging phenotypes change.

Points of disagreement and open questions

Are clocks drivers or passengers?

Most evidence supports clocks as integrated readouts. Some CpGs may be causal or near-causal, but current clocks were generally not built to isolate causal sites. The driver/passenger answer is therefore likely mixed: some clock components may represent damage, others adaptive compensation, and others neutral correlation.

Does lowering epigenetic age rejuvenate organisms?

Not yet established. Interventions that improve health may lower clock measures, but that does not imply the clock change caused the improvement. A decisive test would lower clock age by targeted methylation editing while holding upstream physiology constant, then measure functional aging outcomes.

Which clock is closest to causal aging?

DunedinPACE is arguably closest to an aging-rate phenotype because it is trained on longitudinal physiological change. GrimAge is strongest for mortality prediction. Horvath is strongest for cross-tissue chronological age estimation. None has decisive causal validation.

Are methylation changes damage or adaptation?

This is actively contested. Causality-enriched work argues that ordinary clocks conflate damaging and adaptive methylation changes. If true, blindly reversing epigenetic age could erase compensatory responses as well as damage markers.

Why it matters

DNAm clocks are increasingly used as endpoints in longevity trials, consumer testing, and mechanistic aging studies. If clocks are only biomarkers, then “reversing” a clock may not equal rejuvenation. If some clock components are causal, they could become therapeutic targets. The current evidence supports using clocks as intermediate biomarkers, not as definitive surrogate endpoints for lifespan or healthspan.

Next research steps

  1. Targeted methylation editing: perturb specific clock CpGs or modules and test effects on cellular/tissue aging phenotypes.
  2. Mediation trials: in randomized interventions, test whether clock changes mediate downstream functional benefits.
  3. Hard endpoints: pair clock changes with strength, cognition, immune function, disease incidence, and mortality rather than relying on clock movement alone.
  4. Tissue-specific validation: determine which clocks reflect local tissue aging versus blood/immune-system state.
  5. Separate damage from adaptation: build clocks or subclocks that distinguish harmful methylation drift from compensatory responses.
  6. Pre-registration and replication: especially for intervention studies, pre-register clock endpoints and replicate in independent cohorts.

Overall conclusion

DNAm aging clocks are real and useful biomarkers. They robustly correlate with age, mortality, and physiological decline, and some are responsive to interventions. But the claim that Horvath, PhenoAge, GrimAge, or DunedinPACE directly measure a causal biological aging process is stronger than the evidence allows. The best current position is: clocks measure aging-related biological state and risk; some components may participate causally; many components are likely passengers or adaptive responses; and clock reversal should be treated as suggestive, not definitive, evidence of rejuvenation.

Strengths

  1. Clear causal distinction. The draft repeatedly distinguishes predictive validity from causal validity, which is the central methodological issue.
  2. Good clock-by-clock framing. Horvath, PhenoAge, GrimAge, and DunedinPACE are differentiated by training target and implied interpretation rather than treated as interchangeable.
  3. Appropriate caution on interventions. CALERIE is correctly treated as stronger than uncontrolled studies but still insufficient to prove methylation-clock mediation. TRIIM is appropriately labeled weak due to small, open-label, multi-component design.
  4. Established vs speculative labels are useful. These labels make the report readable and reduce the risk of accidental overclaiming.
  5. Methodological critiques are well chosen. Training targets, confounding, reverse causation, blood cell composition, tissue specificity, heterogeneous CpGs, endpoint circularity, and lack of sufficiency tests are all central critiques.
  6. Conclusion is balanced. The report lands on the defensible position: DNAm clocks measure aging-related state/risk; some components may be causal; clock reversal is suggestive but not definitive rejuvenation.

Main weaknesses / gaps

  1. Citation support is too sparse for a final-grade report. The major source list is credible, but several claims are unsupported in-text. Add citations for:
  • mortality prediction/meta-analysis beyond one Chen/Marioni DOI;
  • cell-type composition effects and immune confounding;
  • tissue-specific clock behavior;
  • intervention evidence for exercise, diet, reprogramming, and animal studies;
  • critiques of epigenetic clocks as surrogate endpoints.
  1. The interventional evidence section needs more granularity. It correctly says evidence is limited, but should separate:
  • randomized human interventions with clock endpoints;
  • uncontrolled human studies;
  • animal/intervention studies;
  • cellular partial reprogramming / methylation-reset models;
  • direct methylation editing evidence, if any.

    • This would make the strength-of-evidence hierarchy more explicit.

  1. Mechanistic evidence is underdeveloped. The report says there are few sufficiency tests, but does not deeply discuss whether methylation changes plausibly regulate gene expression, chromatin state, cellular identity, senescence, inflammation, or stem-cell exhaustion in aging contexts. It should clarify that methylation can be mechanistically causal in some loci while clocks as aggregate predictors are not proven causal.
  1. Need stronger handling of “causal biological aging process.” The draft implies no clock measures a single process, but could explicitly distinguish three possible meanings:
  • biomarker causally downstream of aging damage;
  • mediator in the causal pathway to decline;
  • manipulable surrogate endpoint whose change predicts clinical benefit.

    • The evidence differs for each.

  1. Potential overgeneralization of DunedinPACE. The draft rightly calls DunedinPACE conceptually closer to pace-of-aging, but should mention limitations: cohort derivation, blood DNAm proxy nature, sensitivity to inflammation/exposure, and need for external validation across ages/populations/interventions.
  1. No confidence table by clock. A compact table would materially improve usability: clock, training target, best-supported use, causal-evidence grade, main confounders, interventional responsiveness, bottom-line interpretation.
  1. Limited discussion of negative or inconsistent intervention results. A causal assessment should not only cite interventions that move clocks; it should also discuss variability across clocks, tissues, platforms, time scales, and studies where interventions affect health markers without consistent clock changes or vice versa.

Evidence-strength assessment

The draft's central claim is well supported: DNAm clocks have strong predictive/associational validity and weak-to-limited causal validation. Evidence that clock CpGs are drivers of organismal aging is weak. Evidence that clocks are responsive biomarkers is moderate for some settings, especially randomized caloric restriction effects on DunedinPACE-like measures. Evidence that changing the clock mediates health or lifespan benefits remains speculative.

The report should state even more explicitly that interventional clock reversal is not equivalent to rejuvenation unless paired with functional outcomes and mediation evidence. Current evidence is not enough to validate any of Horvath, PhenoAge, GrimAge, or DunedinPACE as definitive surrogate endpoints for aging interventions.

Bounded next steps for the researcher

  1. Add a clock comparison table with columns: training target, tissue/sample, main predictive claims, causal-evidence grade, main caveats, intervention responsiveness.
  2. Expand the intervention evidence hierarchy into human RCTs, uncontrolled human studies, animal studies, cellular/reprogramming studies, and direct editing/perturbation studies.
  3. Add citations for cell composition, tissue specificity, and surrogate endpoint critiques.
  4. Add a short section: “What evidence would prove causality?” Include necessary/sufficient methylation perturbation, mediation in RCTs, Mendelian/randomization-like designs where applicable, and hard functional endpoints.
  5. Clarify three causal interpretations: downstream marker, mediator/driver, and validated surrogate endpoint.
  6. Include a cautionary paragraph on clock-specific discordance: interventions may move one clock but not another, so “epigenetic age reversal” is clock-dependent.

Verdict

This is a strong draft for a cautious evidence synthesis. It already answers the user’s question at the right level and avoids hype. To become final-quality, it needs denser sourcing, a more explicit hierarchy of causal evidence, and a clock-by-clock confidence table. The evidence for causal-driver status remains weak; the evidence for biomarker/predictive status is strong; interventional evidence is suggestive but not yet sufficient for causal or surrogate-endpoint claims.
